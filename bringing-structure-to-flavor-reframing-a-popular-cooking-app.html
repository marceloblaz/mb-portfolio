Bringing Structure to Flavor: Reframing a Popular Cooking App

I introduced object-oriented UX to a popular cooking app team, helping us move from fragmented features to a shared product mindset — and enabling smarter collaboration across roles.

The problem in a nutshell

McCormick's Flavor Maker is a cooking companion app with over a million installs. It helps users plan meals, watch recipe videos, create shopping lists, and explore new flavors. Despite its popularity, the app's internal structure lacked clarity. Features were often added in isolation, resulting in a disjointed experience for users — and for the team maintaining it.

When I joined the project, I saw an opportunity: not just to improve UX, but to strengthen how we thought about the product itself. Around that time, I began formal training in Object-Oriented UX (OOUX) and saw a perfect fit between its principles and the app's problems.

Impact

While the app still carried legacy issues, my work helped spark a cultural shift in how the team approached feature development and design:

  • I introduced OOUX thinking to cross-functional teammates, leading to stronger collaboration across design, dev, QA, and product.
  • Weekly sessions focused on object relationships replaced siloed decision-making.
  • New features were designed with object integrity and reuse in mind, reducing complexity.
  • I became a more active product voice, not just a UI executor.

The results weren't flashy — they were structural. But they helped reduce unnecessary feature sprawl and allowed us to deliver new releases more thoughtfully and cohesively.

My approach

Identifying the problem

Despite positive app store reviews, the app experience felt inconsistent. Features like recipe videos and recipes were treated as unrelated, despite their clear connection. A button from video → recipe existed — but not the other way around.

The deeper issue: a lack of shared object understanding.

Introducing OOUX

While pursuing OOUX certification, I began applying its principles in the real world. I hosted meetings to introduce the basics of object-first design thinking. It took time to build buy-in, but curiosity and momentum grew.

Enabling collaboration

I invited developers, QA engineers, and managers into a weekly design session. Once we focused on objects, everyone felt more confident contributing. We weren't just building screens — we were designing systems. That shift gave me, and others, a stronger product voice.

Designing with intent

We began rethinking new features not as isolated ideas, but in terms of how they connected to existing objects. We reused where possible, avoided unnecessary complexity, and made more intentional decisions about the app ecosystem.
